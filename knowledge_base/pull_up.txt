EXERCISE: Pull Up
DESCRIPTION: A compound upper-body pulling movement involving elbow flexion and shoulder
             adduction to raise the body from a dead hang to chin-over-bar position.
PRIMARY JOINTS EVALUATED: elbow
SOURCES:
  - Youdas, J.W., Amundson, C.L., Cicero, K.S., Hahn, J.J., Hlynsky, D.A., & Laskowski, E.R. (2010). Surface electromyographic muscle activation patterns and elbow joint motion during a pull-up, chin-up, or perfect-pullup rotational exercise. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 24(12), 3404–3414. https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0b013e3181f1598c
  - Ronai, P., & Scibek, E. (2014). The Pull-Up. Strength & Conditioning Journal, 36(3), 88–90. https://doi.org/10.1519/SSC.0000000000000052
  - Fees, M., Decker, T., Snyder-Mackler, L., & Axe, M.J. (1998). Upper extremity weight-training modifications for the injured athlete. The American Journal of Sports Medicine, 26(5), 732–742. https://doi.org/10.1177/036354659802600521

================================================================================
PHASE: dead_hang
DESCRIPTION: Starting position. Arms fully extended overhead, body hanging freely.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the dead hang position, the elbow angle should be between 155° and 185°,
  indicating full or near-full extension. According to Youdas et al. (2010),
  initiating the pull-up from a full dead hang maximises the range of motion and
  ensures complete activation of the latissimus dorsi through its full length.
  Ronai & Scibek (2014) recommend that the athlete engage the scapular depressors
  before initiating the pull to protect the shoulder girdle under load.
ANGLE RANGES:
  elbow_angle: 155°–185° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Incomplete elbow extension at dead hang
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle < 155°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Failing to fully extend the elbows at the dead hang position reduces the effective
  range of motion of the exercise and places sustained isometric load on the elbow
  flexors. Youdas et al. (2010) demonstrated that EMG activation of the biceps
  brachii and brachialis is significantly elevated when the elbow is maintained in
  partial flexion at the start position, increasing fatigue and injury risk with
  repeated sets. Fees et al. (1998) note that incomplete extension also increases
  anterior shoulder stress by maintaining the humerus in a forward position.
SOURCE: Youdas et al. (2010) JSCR 24(12):3404–3414; Fees et al. (1998) AJSM 26(5):732–742
CORRECTION CUE:
  Let the arms fully straighten at the bottom of every repetition before pulling
  again. Allow a brief pause at the dead hang to confirm full extension.
  Cue: "Dead hang — arms straight, shoulders relaxed, before every rep."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Elbow hyperextension at dead hang
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle > 185°
SEVERITY: danger
MECHANISM:
  Hyperextension of the elbow during a loaded dead hang places excessive stress
  on the ulnar collateral ligament and the posterior elbow capsule. Fees et al.
  (1998) caution that hypermobile athletes are particularly at risk of posterior
  impingement and olecranon stress under the bodyweight load of a dead hang with
  hyperextended elbows. This risk is compounded at higher bodyweights.
SOURCE: Fees et al. (1998) AJSM 26(5):732–742
CORRECTION CUE:
  Maintain a very slight micro-bend in the elbows even at the bottom position.
  Engage the lats slightly before hanging to protect the elbow joint.
  Cue: "Arms long but not locked — keep the elbows soft."

================================================================================
PHASE: top_position
DESCRIPTION: Maximum contraction position. Chin above the bar, elbows deeply flexed.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the top position, the elbow angle should fall between 40° and 75°, indicating
  sufficient elbow flexion to bring the chin above the bar level. Youdas et al.
  (2010) established that peak biceps brachii and latissimus dorsi activation occurs
  in this range. Ronai & Scibek (2014) specify that the chin must clear the bar to
  constitute a complete repetition, which corresponds to approximately 40°–60° of
  elbow flexion depending on arm length.
ANGLE RANGES:
  elbow_angle: 40°–75° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Incomplete pull — elbow insufficiently flexed at top
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle > 75°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Failure to achieve sufficient elbow flexion at the top means the chin does not
  clear the bar, constituting an incomplete repetition. Beyond the technical
  standard, Youdas et al. (2010) showed that peak lat and bicep activation is
  only reached in the upper range of elbow flexion. Stopping short of this range
  consistently under-develops the target musculature and may reflect a strength
  deficit that should be addressed with assisted variations.
SOURCE: Youdas et al. (2010) JSCR 24(12):3404–3414; Ronai & Scibek (2014) SCJ 36(3):88–90
CORRECTION CUE:
  Drive the elbows down and back rather than thinking about pulling the bar down.
  Focus on bringing the chest to the bar, not just the chin.
  Cue: "Elbows to your back pockets — chest to the bar."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Elbow over-flexion at top position
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle < 40°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Extreme elbow flexion beyond the natural top position typically indicates a
  kipping or momentum-driven movement rather than strict pull-up technique.
  Fees et al. (1998) note that rapid elbow flexion under load combined with
  shoulder instability increases anterior shoulder impingement risk. Ronai &
  Scibek (2014) recommend controlled tempo throughout to eliminate momentum.
SOURCE: Fees et al. (1998) AJSM 26(5):732–742; Ronai & Scibek (2014) SCJ 36(3):88–90
CORRECTION CUE:
  Use a controlled 2-second pull and 3-second lowering tempo. Avoid kipping
  or using leg drive to assist the movement.
  Cue: "Pull with your back, not your momentum — control every inch."
