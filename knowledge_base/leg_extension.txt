EXERCISE: Leg Extension
DESCRIPTION: An open kinetic chain (OKC) isolation exercise performed on a machine,
             involving knee extension from a flexed starting position to near-full
             extension against resistance applied at the ankle.
PRIMARY JOINTS EVALUATED: knee
SOURCES:
  - Witvrouw, E., Danneels, L., Van Tiggelen, D., Willems, T.M., & Cambier, D. (2004). Open versus closed kinetic chain exercises in patellofemoral pain: a 5-year prospective randomized study. The American Journal of Sports Medicine, 32(5), 1122–1130. https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546503262177
  - Escamilla, R.F., Fleisig, G.S., Zheng, N., Barrentine, S.W., Wilk, K.E., & Andrews, J.R. (1998). Biomechanics of the knee during closed kinetic chain and open kinetic chain exercises. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 30(4), 556–569. https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-199804000-00014
  - Steinkamp, L.A., Dillingham, M.F., Markel, M.D., Hill, J.A., & Kaufman, K.R. (1993). Biomechanical considerations in patellofemoral joint rehabilitation. The American Journal of Sports Medicine, 21(3), 438–444. https://doi.org/10.1177/036354659302100317

================================================================================
PHASE: start_bent
DESCRIPTION: Starting position with the knee flexed, foot behind the pad.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the starting position, the knee angle should fall between 70° and 109°,
  reflecting a comfortable starting flexion that allows controlled extension
  through the full range. Steinkamp et al. (1993) established that patellofemoral
  compressive forces are elevated at knee flexion angles between 60° and 90°,
  but these forces are brief and acceptable in a slow, controlled movement.
  Witvrouw et al. (2004) specify that a controlled start position is important
  to avoid sudden loading of the patellar tendon at the beginning of the movement,
  particularly in individuals with patellofemoral pain syndrome.
ANGLE RANGES:
  knee_angle: 70°–109° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Excessive knee flexion at start — knee over-bent
ANGLE INDICATOR: knee_angle < 70°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Starting the leg extension with the knee flexed beyond 70° places the
  patellofemoral joint under elevated compressive stress before the movement
  even begins. Steinkamp et al. (1993) demonstrated that patellofemoral
  compressive force is highest at deep flexion angles during open kinetic
  chain movements, with forces peaking near 60° of knee flexion. Escamilla
  et al. (1998) similarly found that tibiofemoral compressive forces are
  significantly elevated in deep knee flexion during OKC exercises compared
  to the mid-range, making a deeply flexed start particularly stressful for
  the patellofemoral joint and patellar tendon.
SOURCE: Steinkamp et al. (1993) AJSM 21(3):438–444; Escamilla et al. (1998) MSSE 30(4):556–569
CORRECTION CUE:
  Adjust the machine so that the starting knee angle is between 80° and 100°.
  Most leg extension machines have a seat adjustment to control starting flexion.
  Begin each repetition from this controlled position rather than a deep bend.
  Cue: "Start with a 90° bend — not deeper. Adjust the seat if needed."

================================================================================
PHASE: extended
DESCRIPTION: Extended position. Leg straightened against the resistance pad.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the extended position, the knee angle should fall between 110° and 175°,
  reflecting near-full extension without hyperextension. Witvrouw et al. (2004)
  note that the leg extension exercise is effective for quadriceps strengthening
  when performed through a controlled range, provided the terminal range is not
  forced into hyperextension. Escamilla et al. (1998) established that anterior
  shear forces at the knee (anterior cruciate ligament stress) are highest during
  OKC extension in the 10°–40° range from full extension, making the final degrees
  of extension the most biomechanically sensitive portion of the movement.
ANGLE RANGES:
  knee_angle: 110°–175° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Knee hyperextension at full extension
ANGLE INDICATOR: knee_angle > 175°
SEVERITY: danger
MECHANISM:
  Forcing the knee into hyperextension at the end of the leg extension movement
  places sudden stress on the posterior knee capsule, the posterior cruciate
  ligament (PCL), and the popliteal structures. Escamilla et al. (1998) found
  that posterior shear forces at the tibiofemoral joint increase significantly
  when the knee moves beyond neutral extension under resistance. Witvrouw et al.
  (2004) specifically caution against terminal hyperextension in the leg extension
  machine, particularly in athletes with patellofemoral pain, as it creates an
  abrupt deceleration load on the patellar tendon at the end range.
  In rehabilitation contexts, Steinkamp et al. (1993) identified hyperextension
  under load as a contraindicated position for patellofemoral rehabilitation.
SOURCE: Escamilla et al. (1998) MSSE 30(4):556–569; Witvrouw et al. (2004) AJSM 32(5):1122–1130; Steinkamp et al. (1993) AJSM 21(3):438–444
CORRECTION CUE:
  Stop the extension just short of full lockout — maintain a very slight bend
  in the knee at the top of the movement. Control the weight through the entire
  range; do not allow the pad to snap the leg into extension.
  Cue: "Extend fully but stop before locking out — keep the muscle under tension."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Incomplete extension — insufficient range of motion
ANGLE INDICATOR: knee_angle < 110°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Failing to extend the knee to at least 110° from the starting position
  represents an incomplete range of motion that under-stimulates the quadriceps
  musculature. Witvrouw et al. (2004) demonstrated that quadriceps strengthening
  benefits from the leg extension are most pronounced when the full available
  range is used, as the vastus medialis oblique (VMO) — critical for patellar
  tracking — is most active in the terminal extension range (final 30°).
  Incomplete extension consistently under-activates the VMO, which can
  contribute to patellar maltracking over time.
SOURCE: Witvrouw et al. (2004) AJSM 32(5):1122–1130
CORRECTION CUE:
  Extend the leg to near-full range on every repetition to ensure complete
  VMO activation. If completing the range is painful, reduce the load rather
  than reducing the range of motion.
  Cue: "Full extension every rep — squeeze the quad at the top."
