EXERCISE: Shoulder Press (Overhead Press)
DESCRIPTION: A compound upper-body pushing movement involving vertical pressing of a
             load from shoulder height to full overhead extension.
PRIMARY JOINTS EVALUATED: elbow
SOURCES:
  - Kolber, M.J., Beekhuizen, K.S., Cheng, M.S., & Hellman, M.A. (2014). Characteristics of anterior shoulder instability and hyperlaxity in the weight-training population. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 28(5), 1213–1217. https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0000000000000415
  - Fees, M., Decker, T., Snyder-Mackler, L., & Axe, M.J. (1998). Upper extremity weight-training modifications for the injured athlete. The American Journal of Sports Medicine, 26(5), 732–742. https://doi.org/10.1177/036354659802600521
  - Dillman, C.J., Murray, T.A., & Hintermeister, R.A. (1994). Biomechanical differences of open and closed chain exercises with respect to the shoulder. Journal of Sport Rehabilitation, 3(3), 228–238. https://doi.org/10.1123/jsr.3.3.228

================================================================================
PHASE: rack_position
DESCRIPTION: Starting position. Bar or dumbbells at shoulder height, elbows forward.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the rack position, the elbow angle should fall between 75° and 100°, reflecting
  the bar resting at clavicle height with the upper arms approximately parallel to
  the floor. Dillman et al. (1994) describe this as the position of maximum shoulder
  joint loading during the press, making correct setup critical for injury prevention.
  Kolber et al. (2014) emphasise that the elbows should be positioned slightly in
  front of the bar, not flared excessively to the sides, to reduce anterior capsule
  stress. The wrists should be stacked directly over the elbows.
ANGLE RANGES:
  elbow_angle: 75°–100° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Elbow angle too acute at rack — elbows too high
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle < 75°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  When the elbows are positioned above the bar at the rack position, the bar path
  is forced forward rather than vertical. Dillman et al. (1994) demonstrate that
  a non-vertical bar path during overhead pressing dramatically increases the
  moment arm at the shoulder, elevating deltoid and rotator cuff demand. Kolber
  et al. (2014) associate this position with increased anterior shoulder instability
  in trained populations, particularly when combined with heavy loads.
SOURCE: Dillman et al. (1994) J. Sport Rehabil. 3(3):228–238; Kolber et al. (2014) JSCR 28(5):1213–1217
CORRECTION CUE:
  Set the bar in the rack at clavicle height. When unracking, keep the elbows
  slightly in front of the bar with the upper arm at approximately 45° to the torso.
  Cue: "Elbows forward, not out — bar over the heels of your hands."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Elbow flare — elbows too wide at rack
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle > 100°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Excessive elbow flare at the rack position abducts the humerus away from the
  torso, placing the shoulder in a position of impingement risk. Fees et al. (1998)
  identify wide elbow positioning during pressing as a primary mechanism for
  subacromial impingement, as the greater tuberosity of the humerus narrows the
  subacromial space when the arm is abducted beyond 90°. Kolber et al. (2014)
  also found elbow flare to be associated with increased anterior instability.
SOURCE: Fees et al. (1998) AJSM 26(5):732–742; Kolber et al. (2014) JSCR 28(5):1213–1217
CORRECTION CUE:
  Bring the elbows slightly forward and in, creating approximately a 45° angle
  between the upper arm and the torso at the start position.
  Cue: "Tuck your elbows — pretend you are bending the bar around your body."

================================================================================
PHASE: lockout
DESCRIPTION: Top position. Arms fully extended overhead, load directly above the base of support.
CORRECT FORM:
  At lockout, the elbow angle should be between 155° and 185°, reflecting full
  or near-full arm extension. Fees et al. (1998) specify that the bar should
  finish directly overhead with the arms vertical when viewed from the front,
  ensuring the load is centred over the body's base of support. Dillman et al.
  (1994) note that full elbow extension at lockout is important for complete
  triceps activation and efficient load transfer through the skeleton.
ANGLE RANGES:
  elbow_angle: 155°–185° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Incomplete lockout — elbow insufficiently extended overhead
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle < 155°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Failing to fully extend the elbows at lockout constitutes an incomplete
  repetition and maintains the load on the musculature rather than transferring
  it through the skeleton. Fees et al. (1998) note that partial lockout during
  overhead pressing places sustained demand on the deltoid and supraspinatus,
  increasing fatigue-related injury risk with high repetitions. Dillman et al.
  (1994) identify incomplete lockout as a factor that prevents the natural
  shoulder rhythm from completing its arc, reducing long-term shoulder health.
SOURCE: Fees et al. (1998) AJSM 26(5):732–742; Dillman et al. (1994) J. Sport Rehabil. 3(3):228–238
CORRECTION CUE:
  Press fully to lockout on every repetition. At the top, shrug the traps
  slightly to elevate the scapula into full upward rotation, completing the
  natural shoulder girdle movement.
  Cue: "Push the ceiling — reach tall at the top of every rep."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Elbow hyperextension at overhead lockout
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle > 185°
SEVERITY: danger
MECHANISM:
  Hyperextension of the elbow under overhead load stresses the posterior elbow
  capsule and the olecranon. The risk is amplified overhead because bodyweight
  and gravitational forces act through the hyperextended joint. Fees et al. (1998)
  explicitly caution athletes with elbow hypermobility against overhead pressing
  without cueing a soft lock, as repeated hyperextension under load can cause
  posterior elbow impingement and olecranon stress reactions.
SOURCE: Fees et al. (1998) AJSM 26(5):732–742
CORRECTION CUE:
  At the top of the press, maintain a very slight bend in the elbows — do not
  snap them to full hyperextension. Think of "long arms" rather than "locked arms."
  Cue: "Soft lock at the top — strong and long, not snapped."
