EXERCISE: Squat
DESCRIPTION: A compound lower-body movement involving simultaneous hip and knee flexion and extension.
PRIMARY JOINTS EVALUATED: knee, hip
SOURCES:
  - Schoenfeld, B.J. (2010). Squatting kinematics and kinetics and their application to exercise performance. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 24(12), 3497–3506. https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0b013e3181bac2d7
  - Escamilla, R.F. (2001). Knee biomechanics of the dynamic squat exercise. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 33(1), 127–141. https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200101000-00020
  - Myer, G.D., Kushner, A.M., Brent, J.L., Schoenfeld, B.J., Hugentobler, J., Lloyd, R.S., ... & McGill, S.M. (2014). The back squat: A proposed assessment of functional deficits and technical factors that limit performance. Strength & Conditioning Journal, 36(6), 4–27. https://doi.org/10.1519/SSC.0000000000000103
  - McGill, S.M. (2015). Back Mechanic. Backfitpro Inc. ISBN: 978-0-9736018-4-3

================================================================================
PHASE: standing
DESCRIPTION: Lockout position at the top of the squat. Both knees and hips fully extended.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the standing lockout position, the knee angle should be between 155° and 185°,
  reflecting full extension without hyperextension. The hip angle should similarly
  reach 155°–185°, indicating complete hip extension. The spine remains neutral with
  the torso upright. According to Schoenfeld (2010), full hip extension at lockout
  is essential for complete gluteal activation and safe load transfer through the
  lumbar spine.
ANGLE RANGES:
  knee_angle: 155°–185° (correct)
  hip_angle:  155°–185° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Knee hyperextension at lockout
ANGLE INDICATOR: knee_angle > 185°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Hyperextension of the knee at lockout places excessive stress on the posterior
  knee capsule, the posterior cruciate ligament (PCL), and the popliteal structures.
  Escamilla (2001) notes that posterior shear forces at the knee increase significantly
  when the joint moves beyond full extension, particularly under load.
SOURCE: Escamilla (2001) Med. Sci. Sports Exerc. 33(1):127–141
CORRECTION CUE:
  Maintain a soft lock at the top — keep a very slight micro-bend in the knees
  at lockout. Think "tall spine, strong legs" rather than snapping the knees back.
  Cue: "Stand tall but do not snap the knees back."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Incomplete hip extension at lockout
ANGLE INDICATOR: hip_angle < 155°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Failure to achieve full hip extension at the top of the squat indicates incomplete
  gluteal contraction and places residual load on the lumbar erectors. McGill (2015)
  describes this as a contributor to chronic low back fatigue during high-volume
  squat training. Schoenfeld (2010) confirms that full hip extension is required
  for optimal posterior chain activation.
SOURCE: Schoenfeld (2010) JSCR 24(12):3497–3506; McGill (2015) Back Mechanic
CORRECTION CUE:
  Squeeze the glutes firmly at the top of every rep. Drive the hips forward and
  through to full extension before descending again.
  Cue: "Squeeze your glutes hard at the top — hips through."

================================================================================
PHASE: bottom_position
DESCRIPTION: Maximum depth position of the squat. Highest demand on knee and hip mobility.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the bottom of the squat, the knee angle should fall between 55° and 100°,
  reflecting a depth at or below parallel. The hip angle should remain between
  55° and 105°, indicating controlled hip flexion without posterior pelvic tilt
  (butt wink). Escamilla (2001) established that patellofemoral compressive forces
  peak between 60° and 90° of knee flexion and are brief in a well-executed squat.
  Myer et al. (2014) emphasise that the torso lean must remain controlled, with
  the spine neutral throughout the descent.
ANGLE RANGES:
  knee_angle: 55°–100° (correct)
  hip_angle:  55°–105° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Butt wink (posterior pelvic tilt / lumbar flexion)
ANGLE INDICATOR: hip_angle < 55°
SEVERITY: danger
MECHANISM:
  Butt wink refers to the rounding of the lower back caused by posterior pelvic
  tilt at the bottom of the squat. When the hip angle drops below 55°, the pelvis
  rotates posteriorly, causing lumbar flexion under compressive load. McGill (2015)
  identifies this as one of the primary mechanisms for lumbar disc injury during
  squatting, as the combination of flexion and axial load significantly increases
  intradiscal pressure at L4/L5 and L5/S1. Schoenfeld (2010) notes that limited
  hip flexor flexibility and ankle dorsiflexion are common underlying causes.
SOURCE: McGill (2015) Back Mechanic; Schoenfeld (2010) JSCR 24(12):3497–3506
CORRECTION CUE:
  Work on hip flexor and ankle dorsiflexion mobility before squatting to depth.
  Use a slightly wider stance with toes turned out to accommodate hip anatomy.
  Elevate heels slightly if ankle mobility is limited.
  Cue: "Sit tall into the hole — chest up, maintain your arch."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Excessive knee flexion depth
ANGLE INDICATOR: knee_angle < 55°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Knee angles below 55° during the squat place extreme compressive loads on the
  patellofemoral joint and menisci. Escamilla (2001) demonstrated that both
  patellofemoral compressive force and tibiofemoral compressive force increase
  substantially at deep knee flexion angles, especially under external load.
  Steinkamp et al. (1993) similarly found peak patellofemoral stress at deep
  flexion angles during open and closed chain movements.
SOURCE: Escamilla (2001) Med. Sci. Sports Exerc. 33(1):127–141
CORRECTION CUE:
  Limit squat depth to the point where neutral spine can be maintained.
  For most athletes, parallel (knee angle ~90°) is sufficient for strength
  development without excessive joint stress.
  Cue: "Squat to parallel — depth means nothing if your back rounds."

--- ERROR 3 ---
NAME: Forward lean / torso collapse
ANGLE INDICATOR: hip_angle < 55° combined with upright torso loss
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Excessive forward lean at the bottom shifts load from the posterior chain
  to the lumbar spine. Myer et al. (2014) identify forward lean as a technical
  deficit that increases spinal extensor demand and reduces mechanical efficiency.
  McGill (2015) notes that sustained lumbar flexion under compressive load is
  the primary disc injury mechanism in squatting.
SOURCE: Myer et al. (2014) SCJ 36(6):4–27; McGill (2015) Back Mechanic
CORRECTION CUE:
  Drive your elbows down and back to keep the bar path vertical.
  Keep your chest up throughout the descent.
  Cue: "Lead with your chest, not your hips."
