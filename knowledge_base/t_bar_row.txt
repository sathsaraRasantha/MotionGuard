EXERCISE: T-Bar Row
DESCRIPTION: A compound upper-body pulling movement performed in a hip-hinged position,
             targeting the latissimus dorsi, rhomboids, and middle trapezius while
             maintaining a stable lumbar spine under horizontal load.
PRIMARY JOINTS EVALUATED: elbow, hip
SOURCES:
  - Fenwick, C.M.J., Brown, S.H.M., & McGill, S.M. (2009). Comparison of different rowing exercises: trunk muscle activation and lumbar spine motion, load, and stiffness. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 23(5), 1408–1417. https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0b013e3181b03480
  - McGill, S.M. (2015). Back Mechanic. Backfitpro Inc. ISBN: 978-0-9736018-4-3
  - Signorile, J.F., Zink, A.J., & Szwed, S.P. (2002). A comparative electromyographical investigation of muscle utilization patterns using various hand positions during the lat pull-down. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 16(4), 539–546. https://doi.org/10.1519/1533-4287(2002)016<0539>

================================================================================
PHASE: start_position
DESCRIPTION: Arms fully extended, hip hinge established. Load hanging at arm's length.
CORRECT FORM:
  At the start position, the elbow angle should be between 150° and 185°, reflecting
  full arm extension. The hip angle should fall between 80° and 145°, establishing
  the hip hinge with the torso between 45° and parallel to the floor. Fenwick et al.
  (2009) measured lumbar spine mechanics across rowing variations and found that
  the hip hinge angle at setup is the primary determinant of spinal load throughout
  the entire set. McGill (2015) specifies that the lumbar spine must be in neutral
  lordosis — not flexed — at the start position, as any pre-existing lumbar flexion
  dramatically increases L4/L5 disc loading under the rowing load.
ANGLE RANGES:
  elbow_angle: 150°–185° (correct)
  hip_angle:   80°–145° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Insufficient hip hinge — torso too upright at start
ANGLE INDICATOR: hip_angle > 145°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  When the hip angle exceeds 145° at the start of the T-bar row, the torso is
  too upright for the horizontal pulling movement. This shifts the exercise from
  a horizontal row (targeting mid-back) toward a vertical pull, reducing
  latissimus dorsi and rhomboid activation. Signorile et al. (2002) demonstrated
  that body position is a primary determinant of muscle recruitment during rowing
  movements. An upright torso also places the bar path in front of the centre of
  mass, increasing anterior shoulder demand unnecessarily.
SOURCE: Signorile et al. (2002) JSCR 16(4):539–546; Fenwick et al. (2009) JSCR 23(5):1408–1417
CORRECTION CUE:
  Hinge forward at the hips until the torso is between 45° and parallel to the
  floor before gripping the bar. Keep the chest up and maintain a neutral spine
  throughout the hinge.
  Cue: "Hinge — push your hips back, chest forward, torso parallel."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Excessive forward lean — torso too horizontal
ANGLE INDICATOR: hip_angle < 80°
SEVERITY: danger
MECHANISM:
  A hip angle below 80° places the torso nearly parallel to or past horizontal,
  which substantially increases the compressive and shear forces on the lumbar
  spine. Fenwick et al. (2009) measured peak L4/L5 compressive forces during
  various rowing exercises and found that more horizontal torso positions
  generated forces approaching 3,000N — well above safe repetitive loading
  thresholds identified by McGill (2015). The risk is compounded when the
  lumbar spine rounds (flexes) at this angle, which McGill (2015) identifies
  as the primary mechanism for lumbar disc herniation during rowing.
SOURCE: Fenwick et al. (2009) JSCR 23(5):1408–1417; McGill (2015) Back Mechanic
CORRECTION CUE:
  Raise the torso slightly by extending at the hips. Maintain a neutral lumbar
  curve — do not allow the lower back to round. Consider reducing load if
  maintaining position is difficult.
  Cue: "Chest up, back flat — your spine should look like a tabletop."

--- ERROR 3 ---
NAME: Incomplete arm extension at start
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle < 150°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Failing to fully extend the elbows at the start position of the row reduces
  the range of motion and places sustained load on the elbow flexors and bicep
  tendon. Fenwick et al. (2009) note that full arm extension at the start of
  rowing movements is necessary to achieve full scapular protraction, which in
  turn maximises rhomboid and mid-trapezius activation through the full range
  of the pulling movement.
SOURCE: Fenwick et al. (2009) JSCR 23(5):1408–1417
CORRECTION CUE:
  Fully straighten the arms at the bottom of every repetition. Allow the
  shoulder blades to protract (spread apart) at the bottom before initiating
  the next pull.
  Cue: "Let your shoulder blades spread apart at the bottom — feel the stretch."

================================================================================
PHASE: full_contraction
DESCRIPTION: Maximum contraction position. Bar at chest/lower sternum, elbows behind the body.
CORRECT FORM:
  At full contraction, the elbow angle should fall between 50° and 80°, reflecting
  the upper arms positioned behind the torso. The hip angle should remain between
  80° and 145°, indicating that the hip hinge position is maintained throughout
  the pulling movement. Fenwick et al. (2009) emphasise that the torso position
  must remain stable through the concentric phase — any torso rise or lurch at
  contraction indicates the load is excessive. Signorile et al. (2002) found
  maximum rhomboid and mid-trapezius activation when the elbows are driven
  past the torso line at the top of the rowing movement.
ANGLE RANGES:
  elbow_angle: 50°–80° (correct)
  hip_angle:   80°–145° (correct)
================================================================================

--- ERROR 1 ---
NAME: Elbow over-flexion at contraction — excessive bicep curl
ANGLE INDICATOR: elbow_angle < 50°
SEVERITY: warning
MECHANISM:
  Elbow angles below 50° at the top of the row indicate the athlete is
  curling the bar past the natural endpoint of the rowing movement. This
  shifts the primary demand from the back musculature to the bicep brachii,
  reducing the training stimulus for the intended target muscles. Fenwick
  et al. (2009) note that over-pulling at the top of rowing movements also
  causes the shoulder to internally rotate, which can increase bicipital
  tendon stress at the anterior shoulder.
SOURCE: Fenwick et al. (2009) JSCR 23(5):1408–1417
CORRECTION CUE:
  Stop the pull when the bar reaches the lower sternum and the elbows are
  just behind the torso. Focus on squeezing the shoulder blades together
  rather than pulling the bar further up.
  Cue: "Squeeze your shoulder blades together at the top — the bar stops at your chest."

--- ERROR 2 ---
NAME: Hip angle collapse during row — torso rising during pull
ANGLE INDICATOR: hip_angle outside 80°–145° at contraction
SEVERITY: danger
MECHANISM:
  If the hip angle changes significantly between the start and contraction phases,
  the athlete is using lumbar extension or torso rise to assist the pull rather
  than pure arm and back strength. McGill (2015) describes this as one of the
  most common and dangerous errors in rowing, as the repeated lumbar extension
  under load creates compressive fatigue of the posterior elements and increases
  facet joint stress. Fenwick et al. (2009) measured significantly higher lumbar
  compressive forces in rowing variations where torso movement was used to
  assist the pull compared to strict technique.
SOURCE: Fenwick et al. (2009) JSCR 23(5):1408–1417; McGill (2015) Back Mechanic
CORRECTION CUE:
  Brace the core hard before each rep and maintain the same torso angle from
  start to finish. If the torso rises to complete the rep, reduce the load.
  Cue: "Lock your hips — only your arms move. Your back is a bridge, not a crane."
